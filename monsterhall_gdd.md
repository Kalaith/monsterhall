# Monsterhall - Game Design Document

## High Concept
**Monsterhall** is a fantasy management and dungeon-support game about running a guild hall beside a tower too lethal for ordinary adventurers to explore alone. The player recruits and raises monster companions, assigns them to guild jobs, supports expeditions into the tower, and grows a humble outpost into the only guild capable of surviving the depths.

Monsterfolk are not enemies to clear away. They are the reason exploration is finally possible.

A slime can slip through cracks and retrieve alchemical residue. A lamia can read posture, calm tense clients, and manage reception. A minotaur can bodyguard a party through collapsed corridors. Different monster companions matter both in town and in the tower.

---

## Genre
- Management sim
- Dungeon support / expedition strategy
- Guild builder
- Light creature collection and staff progression

## Target Feel
The game should feel like:
- building a strange but lovable fantasy workplace
- managing specialized staff with distinct strengths
- preparing for dangerous delves rather than directly controlling combat
- growing a scrappy guild into a respected institution

The tone should mix:
- cozy guild management
- dangerous tower mystery
- characterful monsterfolk fantasy
- practical logistics and progression

---

## Pillars

### 1. Monster Companions Matter
Every monster species should solve different problems in town and in the dungeon.

### 2. The Tower Is Genuinely Dangerous
The tower is not just a loot menu. Sending the wrong team, underpreparing, or ignoring risk should cost time, resources, and staff readiness.

### 3. Town Work Supports Delving
The guild survives by balancing expedition work with inn, reception, crafting, training, and contract support jobs.

### 4. Staff Have Personality and Identity
Companions are not faceless units. They have roles, traits, fatigue, stress, and standout uses.

### 5. Progress Comes From Unlocking Capability
The player should feel like they are slowly solving the tower by building better infrastructure, recruiting broader monster types, and improving team support.

---

## Player Fantasy
The player is the guildmaster of **Monsterhall**, a guild hall established at the edge of a deadly tower no ordinary party can reliably survive. Monster companions, long ignored or misunderstood, possess the traits needed to navigate the tower's hazards.

The player builds a mixed workforce of monsterfolk and support staff, creating a guild where strange talents become the key to survival and prosperity.

The fantasy is not "be the strongest fighter." It is:
- assemble the right people
- build the right support systems
- send the right team
- turn impossible exploration into a sustainable business

---

## Core Gameplay Loop

### Daily Loop
1. Review the guild's current needs.
2. Check debt, upkeep, contracts, and readiness.
3. Assign monster companions to town roles, guild services, and expedition support.
4. Hatch or recruit new companions when available.
5. Build or upgrade guild structures.
6. Plan one or more tower expeditions.
7. End the day and resolve outcomes.
8. Spend rewards, recover staff, and prepare for the next day.

### Long-Term Loop
- expand the guild hall
- unlock more rooms and jobs
- recruit new monster species
- push deeper into the tower
- gain better loot, relics, and contracts
- survive harsher debt and upkeep pressures
- become the definitive guild of the tower frontier

---

## Game Structure
The game is divided into interconnected management screens and a day-based schedule.

### Major Areas
- **Town Overview**: current priorities, roster snapshot, resources
- **Guild Hall Management**: room upgrades, support structures, progression
- **Companion Management**: inspect staff, traits, readiness, role fit
- **Contract Desk**: manage incoming jobs and patron requests
- **Expedition Planning**: pick floor, objective, priority, and assigned team
- **Hatchery / Nursery**: hatch eggs or recruit new companions
- **Settings / Journal**: meta functions, lore, discovered floors, species notes

---

## Core Systems

## 1. Companion System
Companions are the heart of the game.

### Companion Attributes
Each companion has:
- Name
- Species
- Role / current assignment
- Trait(s)
- Fatigue
- Stress
- Injury
- Corruption / instability
- Skills
- Best next use

### Example Skills
- Scouting
- Guarding
- Recovery
- Hospitality
- Bargaining
- Crafting
- Charm
- Navigation
- Arcana
- Strength

### Role Fit
Companions are more effective when assigned to work suited to their species and traits.

Examples:
- **Slime**: cleanup, alchemy residue recovery, bar work, narrow tunnel scouting
- **Lamia**: reception, negotiation, calming guests, trap-sense, route memory
- **Minotaur**: escort duty, heavy hauling, guarding, obstacle clearing
- **Harpy**: lookout duty, courier work, vertical scouting
- **Imp**: quick errands, petty craft support, risky scouting
- **Golemkin**: maintenance, smithy assistance, safe frontline support

### Readiness States
Each companion should clearly show whether they are:
- Ready to work
- Resting
- Assigned to town duty
- Assigned to expedition duty
- Recovering from injury
- Too stressed to safely deploy

---

## 2. Town Jobs
Not every companion goes into the tower. Town roles create income, stability, and better expedition outcomes.

### Possible Jobs
- Bar staff
- Reception desk
- Porter / caravan support
- Training assistant
- Recovery room staff
- Alchemy support
- Forge helper
- Archive assistant
- Stable / packroom support
- Contract runner

### Purpose of Town Jobs
Town jobs should:
- generate steady resources
- satisfy contracts and patrons
- reduce stress or fatigue in the roster
- improve expedition preparation
- make unused companions still valuable

---

## 3. Contract / Patron Request System
Local adventurers, traders, scholars, nobles, and travelers bring requests to Monsterhall.

### Contract Types
- escort preparation
- supply packing
- alchemical gathering
- rare ingredient recovery
- reception and lodging support
- tower guide services
- translation or archive research
- beast handling
- scouting commissions

### Contract Design Goals
Contracts should:
- give a reason to care about staff diversity
- create short-term priorities
- generate gold and reputation
- sometimes request specific species or specialties

Examples:
- A scholar wants a slime assistant to safely retrieve corrosive residue.
- A caravan wants a minotaur escort for heavy cargo.
- A nervous delver pays more for a lamia receptionist and route-planning consultation.

---

## 4. Tower Expeditions
Expeditions are planned, not directly controlled. The player assembles a support team and chooses how the run should be approached.

### Expedition Inputs
- selected floor
- mission type
- priority stance
- assigned companions
- readiness and injury state
- preparation quality

### Mission Types
- Resource Run
- Scout Route
- Egg Hunt
- Relic Recovery
- Contract Fulfilment
- Rescue / Retrieval

### Priority Stances
- Safe
- Balanced
- Aggressive
- Recovery-Focused
- Curiosity / Discovery

### Expedition Outputs
- materials
- relics
- eggs
- arcane residue
- injuries
- stress
- discoveries
- floor notes
- special events

### Expedition Risks
- fatigue accumulation
- injury
- stress spikes
- lost time
- corrupted finds
- failed objectives

The tower should feel like a place of mounting unknowns, not a guaranteed reward fountain.

---

## 5. Hatchery / Recruitment
New companions enter the guild through eggs, rescues, contracts, and rare recruitment opportunities.

### Sources of New Staff
- eggs found in expeditions
- rescued tower inhabitants
- wandering applicants
- story events
- faction rewards

### Hatchery Goals
The hatchery should:
- provide anticipation and choice
- tie species growth to tower exploration
- create meaningful roster expansion

### Recruitment Design
Early game should focus on a small number of dependable species. Later game unlocks more specialized and unusual monsterfolk.

---

## 6. Guild Hall Building System
The player expands **Monsterhall** by building rooms that improve staff output, readiness, or tower capability.

### Building Categories
- **Core**: essential infrastructure
- **Service**: income and contract fulfilment
- **Training**: skill growth and specialization
- **Recovery**: fatigue, stress, injury management
- **Research**: discovery, lore, tower intel
- **Expedition Support**: prep quality, safer runs, better loot odds

### Example Buildings
- Common Room
- Reception Hall
- Slime Bar
- Nursery
- Forge Corner
- Alchemy Bench
- Recovery Baths
- Archive Desk
- Watch Post
- Route Map Chamber
- Packroom
- Beast Kennel

### Example Effects
- increase contract income
- unlock new job types
- reduce expedition prep cost
- improve hatch speed
- unlock new species pools
- reduce injury chance
- increase scouting success

---

## 7. Economy
The economy should balance immediate survival with growth planning.

### Main Resources
- **Gold**: wages, debt, construction, purchases
- **Materials**: repairs, building, equipment support
- **Relics**: rare upgrades, research, faction value
- **Essence / Residue**: tower-derived special resource for advanced projects
- **Eggs**: future staff growth

### Expenses
- debt payments
- upkeep
- food and supplies
- recovery costs
- hatch costs
- expedition prep costs
- special projects

### Economic Goal
The player should not become rich by doing only one thing. A healthy run should require some mix of:
- contracts
- town jobs
- expeditions
- strategic building

---

## 8. Debt and Pressure
Debt gives urgency and structure to the campaign.

### Pressure Sources
- periodic debt due
- rising upkeep as roster and facilities expand
- contract deadlines
- tower opportunities that expire
- faction expectations

### Design Intent
Pressure should push decision-making without becoming constant punishment.

Questions the player should ask:
- Can I afford another building now?
- Should I rest my best scout or send one more run?
- Should I take stable contract money or risk a relic expedition?

---

## Progression

### Early Game
- one tiny guild hall
- a few starter companions
- shallow tower floors
- basic town jobs
- survival-focused economy

### Mid Game
- multiple job rooms
- broader species mix
- more dangerous expeditions
- contracts with special requirements
- meaningful staff specialization

### Late Game
- elite guild infrastructure
- advanced floors and hazards
- rare monster companions
- faction-level contracts
- story revelations about the tower
- major strategic choices around risk, expansion, and mastery

---

## World and Setting
Monsterhall sits near a tower long considered a deathtrap. Generations of adventurers have tried to conquer it and failed. The guild's breakthrough is simple but radical: monsterfolk are not just dungeon inhabitants, they are the best possible guides, workers, and companions for surviving its hazards.

The wider world should treat this as unusual, practical, and slightly controversial.

This gives room for:
- prejudice and reputation arcs
- local politics
- pragmatic partnerships
- unusual clients drawn by the guild's success

---

## Tone and Presentation
The tone should be warm, mysterious, and a little dangerous.

### Tone Mix
- 50% cozy fantasy workplace
- 30% dungeon danger and mystery
- 20% character humor and species charm

The game should feel inviting, but the tower must remain credible.

---

## Example Starter Species

### Slime
- Best at cleanup, residue handling, squeeze-through scouting, casual hospitality
- Low cost, high flexibility

### Lamia
- Best at reception, memory, calm negotiation, route guidance
- Good contract and desk utility

### Minotaur
- Best at carrying, guarding, escorting, clearing hazards
- Strong expedition backbone unit

### Harpy
- Best at lookout roles, speed, fetching, vertical traversal

### Imp
- Best at errands, risky poking, light support, nuisance-solving

---

## MVP Scope
A reasonable MVP should include:
- 3 to 4 starter species
- 1 town overview screen
- 1 guild management screen
- 1 companion profile screen
- 1 hatchery screen
- 1 contract desk screen
- 1 expedition planning screen
- 3 to 5 building types
- 2 to 3 tower floors
- basic debt and upkeep
- basic event system

### MVP Success Condition
The player should be able to:
- run the guild for multiple days
- assign companions to town or expedition work
- hatch new companions
- complete contracts
- build facilities
- send expeditions and receive results
- feel that different monster species matter mechanically

---

## Expansion Opportunities
Future versions can add:
- factions and guild reputation
- named adventurer parties
- more species and hybrid roles
- rival guilds
- weather or seasonal pressures
- tower bosses and floor guardians
- deeper companion relationships and story scenes
- gear and artifact loadouts

---

## Design Summary
**Monsterhall** is about turning monster companions from assumed threats into the foundation of a thriving frontier guild. The player grows a mixed monster workforce, balances town and tower priorities, and gradually proves that the tower can only be explored by embracing the talents everyone else ignored.

Its strongest promise is clear:

**Run the only guild capable of surviving the tower, because your monster companions make the impossible practical.**
